Support Frame

Structural mounting for the VINDSNURR 1000 mm rotor

Design status: No support frame has been built or tested for this rotor. All structural recommendations below are theoretical, based on standard timber and steel construction principles applied to the known rotor dimensions and estimated loads. Have structural choices reviewed by a qualified engineer before outdoor installation.

1. What the frame must do

The support frame has four jobs:

  1. Hold the shaft vertically — two bearing housings, one at the top of the rotor zone and one at the bottom, must be coaxial to within ~1 mm over the full shaft length or the rotor will wobble under load.
  2. Carry the rotor weight — the completed rotor (hubs, rods, sails, shaft) is estimated at 3–8 kg depending on materials. The lower bearing takes axial (thrust) load.
  3. Resist overturning moment — wind on the rotor produces a lateral force. At 10 m/s wind on a 1 m² projected area, drag force is roughly 60 N (13 lbf). This acts at the rotor's centre of gravity, which is ~1 m or more above the ground bearing.
  4. Provide clearance for rotation — the rotor sweeps a 1000 mm diameter circle. The frame must not enter this zone.

3. Timber frame — 2×4 vs 4×4

A simple timber A-frame or post-and-crossbar structure is the most accessible option for a first prototype. The question is whether 2×4 (38×89 mm) or 4×4 (89×89 mm) lumber is sufficient.

For the 1000 mm rotor: 2×4 is marginal, 4×4 is appropriate

Section2×4 (38×89 mm)4×4 (89×89 mm)
Vertical post (3 m tall)Adequate if well-braced; prone to racking in windComfortable margin; self-bracing against racking
Horizontal crossbarsAdequate for rotor weight loadsMore than adequate
Bearing mount plateAdequate with metal plate bridgingDirectly accepts bolt-down bearing housings
Wind load resistanceRequires diagonal bracing on all facesRequires bracing on two faces (depth helps)
Outdoor durabilityAdequate with treated lumber (ACQ or similar)Better; more wood to lose before structural failure

Recommendation for 1000 mm rotor: Use 4×4 posts for vertical members. 2×4 is acceptable for horizontal crossbars and diagonal bracing. If the rotor is installed above ~1.5 m off the ground, add at least two diagonal braces (one per axis) to resist overturning.

For larger rotors

If the rotor diameter increases, so do the loads — roughly with the square of diameter for wind force, and with the cube of diameter for the swept area power capture. As a rough guide:

Key dimensions for this rotor:

  • Crossbar height above ground: at least 400 mm (rotor B node must clear the ground when rotor is spinning)
  • Crossbar span: at least 800 mm between posts to provide stability against overturning
  • Post height: crossbar height + rotor height (1064 mm) + upper overhang (300 mm) + bearing plate depth (~50 mm) = ~1800 mm minimum above crossbar
  • Total post length including footing: ~2500–3000 mm

5. Steel tube alternative

For a cleaner installation or where timber is impractical, steel rectangular hollow section (RHS) or round hollow section (RHS) is a better choice:

ComponentSteel specificationNotes
Vertical mast50×50×3 mm RHS or 50 mm OD round tubeSingle central mast; requires guy wires or outriggers
Bearing mount plate6 mm flat plate, laser cut or weldedDrill to accept flanged bearing housing
Outriggers (if mast)40×40×3 mm RHS, 600 mm long, 120° spacing3 outriggers gives stable tripod base
Guy wires (if tall mast)3 mm stainless wire, turnbuckle tensioners2 levels of guys if mast exceeds 3 m

A single steel mast with three outriggers welded at 120° spacing at the base is compact, strong, and leaves the rotor unobstructed on all sides. This is the preferred form for a production installation.

6. Bearing hardware — specification, sourcing, and installation

The bearing

The rotor shaft (20 mm OD aluminium tube) runs in two 6204-2RS deep-groove ball bearings:

ParameterValue
Designation6204-2RS (also written 6204-RS, 6204-2RSH)
Bore (ID)20 mm — matches shaft OD exactly
Outer diameter (OD)47 mm
Width14 mm
Seal type2RS = rubber sealed both sides — important for outdoor use; keeps grease in and water out
Load rating (dynamic)~13 kN (well above rotor loads)
Quantity needed2 (one at top of rotor zone, one at bottom)

Where to source: 6204-2RS is one of the most common bearing sizes in the world. Available from:

  • Amazon / eBay — search "6204-2RS bearing" — $3–$8 each for off-brand, $8–$15 for branded (SKF, NSK, FAG). At this price the risk of buying Chinese no-name bearings for a prototype is acceptable.
  • Local bearing suppliers (e.g. Motion Industries, Grainger in North America; BSC Bearing, CBC in Australia; Simply Bearings, HPC Bearings in the UK) — often $5–$12 each, same-day pickup, guaranteed specification.
  • Robot/RC/maker suppliers (ServoCity, VXB, Amazon) — usually stocked, reasonable quality.

Search terms: "6204-2RS", "6204 RS bearing", "20mm bore bearing". The "2RS" suffix is important — open bearings (6204 with no suffix) are not suitable outdoors.

Bearing housings

The bearing must be held in a housing that bolts to the frame. Two formats:

Pillow block (UCP204)"UCP204 pillow block" or "P204 bearing block"$5–$15 eachBolts flat to a horizontal surface. Upper bearing: to underside of top crossbar. Lower bearing: to top of bottom crossbar. Self-aligning insert tolerates slight misalignment.

All of the UCP204 / UCF204 family use the same 6204 insert bearing. The housing letter changes the mount geometry, not the bearing spec. All include a set screw that locks the housing to the shaft — tighten this only after alignment is confirmed.

Where to source housings: Same suppliers as the bearings. Search "UCP204" or "UCF204". Budget $10–$30 for both housings.

Installation and alignment

  1. Bolt the lower bearing housing to the frame first.
  2. Slide the shaft through the lower bearing.
  3. With the shaft plumb (confirmed with a spirit level), position the upper bearing housing loosely — do not tighten.
  4. Slide the upper bearing down the shaft to its housing and verify the shaft is still plumb.
  5. Tighten the upper housing bolts, then tighten both set screws.
  6. Spin the shaft by hand — it should rotate with no binding and only light resistance from the seals. Any roughness suggests misalignment; loosen and re-set.

Critical: The two bearing housings must be coaxial — both shaft centrelines must align to within 1 mm over the full shaft length. Even 2–3 mm of misalignment will cause the rotor to wobble under load and will shorten bearing life dramatically.

6b. Shaft-to-generator connection

The shaft transfers rotor torque to the generator. There are three common approaches, depending on whether the generator is mounted above or below the rotor.

Generator position options

PositionProsCons
Below lower bearingWeight low (stability), easy access for wiringWiring must pass down through frame base
Above upper bearingClean wiring run down the mastWeight high; harder to service
Inline (direct drive)No coupling needed — shaft is the generator shaftGenerator must be designed for this; rare in off-the-shelf units

For a first build: below the lower bearing is simplest. The rotor shaft extends 200–300 mm below the lower bearing housing, and the generator mounts to the frame directly beneath it.

Coupling the shaft to the generator

The VINDSNURR shaft is 20 mm OD aluminium tube. Most generators and motors have a solid keyed shaft — typically 8 mm, 10 mm, or 12 mm diameter. A coupling is needed to connect them.

Coupling typeSearch termApprox. costNotes
Rigid shaft coupler"rigid shaft coupling 20mm to 10mm"$5–$15Simplest. Zero slip. Requires near-perfect shaft alignment — any misalignment causes vibration and bearing wear. Acceptable for direct drive on a well-aligned frame.
Jaw/spider coupler (flexible)"flexible jaw coupling 20mm 10mm"$8–$25Polyurethane spider insert absorbs minor misalignment and vibration. Preferred over rigid. Search "Oldham coupling" or "jaw coupling".
Belt drive pulley"timing pulley 20mm bore" + "GT2 belt"$10–$30For step-up ratio (e.g. 3:1 to spin generator faster). Allows spatial offset between shaft and generator. GT2 timing belts are low-slip and widely available from 3D printer suppliers.
Friction disc—DIYSimple but slips under load. Not recommended for sustained power generation.

Note: The VINDSNURR shaft is tube, not solid rod. A standard shaft coupler bored to 20 mm will clamp the tube's OD, but if the tube wall is thin (typically 2 mm), the set screw may crush it. Options:

  • Use a collar clamp (split clamp style) rather than a set-screw coupler — distributes clamping force around the full circumference.
  • Fill the tube end with a short aluminium plug (20 mm OD solid rod, 30–40 mm long) epoxied inside before fitting the coupler — gives the set screw a solid surface to bite.
  • Use a 3D-printed adaptor that fits over the tube OD and presents a standard solid shaft diameter to the coupler.

Generator mounting

The generator must be rigidly fixed to the frame — it must not rotate with the shaft. Common approaches:

  • Clamp bracket: a U-bolt or band clamp around the generator body, bolted to a crossbar on the frame. Works for cylindrical generators (outrunner motors, hub motors).
  • Flange mount: generators with a front face plate bolt directly to a plate welded or bolted to the frame. Cleanest option.
  • 3D-printed motor mount: design a mount that accepts the generator's bolt circle and bolts to the frame crossbar. Suitable for prototype phase; upgrade to metal for permanent installation.

Ensure the generator body is electrically isolated from the metal frame if the frame is earthed — especially for permanent outdoor installation. A rubber gasket under the mount bracket is sufficient for low-voltage systems (<48V).

7. Wind and safety loads

Estimated structural loads for the 1000 mm rotor at 15 m/s (storm conditions):

LoadEstimateBasis
Rotor weight (static)5–8 kgHubs, rods, shaft, sails
Wind drag on rotor at 15 m/s~130 N (29 lbf)Cd ≈ 1.0, A = 1 m², ρ = 1.2 kg/m³
Overturning moment at base (1.5 m height)~200 N·m130 N × 1.5 m moment arm
Shaft torsional load at rated power~3–6 N·m at 150 RPMP = τ × ω; P ≈ 50 W typical

These loads are modest for timber or steel construction at this scale. The overturning moment governs the footing design — a 200 N·m overturning moment at the base requires either a heavy ballast (>100 kg) or ground anchors.

8. Scaling up

If the rotor design is validated and scaled to a larger diameter, every structural dimension changes. Key scaling relationships:

  • Wind force on rotor: scales with diameter² (doubling diameter = 4× the force)
  • Overturning moment: scales with diameter³ (doubling diameter = 8× the moment, because both force and height increase)
  • Rotor weight: scales roughly with diameter² to diameter³ depending on material choices
  • Shaft diameter: must increase with rotor weight and torque — 20 mm is appropriate for the 1000 mm rotor; a 1500 mm rotor would likely want 25–30 mm

For rotors above 1500 mm diameter, consult a structural engineer before committing to a frame design. The loads begin to exceed what can be confidently handled by rule-of-thumb timber sizing.

9. Rooftop installation considerations

The VINDSNURR's omnidirectional operation and compact footprint make rooftop installation appealing. Additional considerations:

  • Vibration transmission: any rotor imbalance will transmit vibration into the roof structure. Always balance the rotor before permanent installation (see Assembly Guide §8). Rubber isolation mounts between frame and roof help.
  • Roof load capacity: check that the roof can carry the frame weight plus wind uplift loads before installation. Most pitched roofs can handle localised point loads of 500–1000 N without modification; flat roofs vary.
  • Planning permission: in most jurisdictions, rooftop wind turbines require planning permission or at least notification. Check local rules before installing.
  • Height advantage: wind speed increases with height. Mounting the rotor at 3–5 m above the roofline rather than at roof level can significantly increase output at the same wind site.

All structural recommendations in this document are theoretical estimates based on standard engineering principles. They have not been validated by physical testing or engineering review. Treat them as starting points for design, not as certified specifications. For any permanent outdoor installation, have the frame design reviewed by a qualified structural engineer.
