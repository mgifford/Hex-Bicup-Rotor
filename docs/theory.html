Design Theory and Aerodynamic Background

HBR-6-14V · Vertical-axis drag turbine (Savonius class)

1. The Savonius Turbine — Operating Principle

The VINDSNURR rotor belongs to the Savonius class of vertical-axis wind turbines (VAWTs). Savonius turbines are drag-type devices, not lift-type. This distinction determines nearly every aspect of their performance and geometry.

How drag-based rotation works

A drag turbine works by presenting an asymmetric cross-section to the wind:

  • The concave (cup) face of each blade catches wind and experiences high drag — it is pushed away from the wind source.
  • The convex (back) face sheds wind and experiences much lower drag.
  • The net difference in drag force across the two sides produces a torque about the vertical shaft.

Because no aerodynamic lift is involved, the blade tip can never travel faster than the wind itself. This imposes a fundamental ceiling on performance, but it also means the rotor is self-starting at any wind direction, requires no yaw mechanism, and produces useful torque at very low wind speeds.

The Savonius principle was invented by Finnish engineer Sigurd Johannes Savonius around 1922 (patented in Finland 1926, USA 1925 and 1928). The classic form uses two hemicylindrical buckets arranged in an S-shape when viewed from above.

2. Performance Characteristics

Power coefficient

All wind turbines are bounded by the Betz limit: no rotor can extract more than 16/27 ≈ 59.3% of the kinetic energy in the wind passing through its swept area.

Savonius rotors achieve a practical power coefficient (Cp) of approximately 0.20 — about 20% of available wind energy. For comparison:

Turbine typeTypical Cp
Modern horizontal-axis wind turbine (HAWT)~0.45
Darrieus vertical-axis (lift-based)~0.35
Savonius drag-based~0.20
Cup anemometer~0.08

Tip-speed ratio

The tip-speed ratio (λ) is the ratio of blade tip velocity to wind velocity:

λ = (ω × r) / v

For Savonius turbines, λ ≈ 1. For the VINDSNURR with r = 0.5 m at v = 10 m/s:

ω ≈ 10 / 0.5 = 20 rad/s ≈ 191 RPM

Power output estimate

P_max ≈ 0.12 × h × d × v³

Where h = rotor height (m), d = diameter (m), v = wind speed (m/s). The 0.12 coefficient absorbs air density (1.2 kg/m³) and Cp ≈ 0.20.

For the VINDSNURR (h = 1.064 m, d = 1.0 m):

Wind speedEstimated power output
5 m/s (18 km/h, light breeze)~16 W
7 m/s (25 km/h, moderate breeze)~44 W
10 m/s (36 km/h, fresh breeze)~128 W
12 m/s (43 km/h, strong breeze)~221 W

These are first-order estimates using the 0.20 Cp baseline. Actual output depends on sail coverage fraction, hub interference losses, generator efficiency, and bearing friction.

3. Why a Hexagonal Bicupola Geometry?

Multi-stage torque smoothing

A standard 2-bucket Savonius produces strongly pulsed torque. The VINDSNURR achieves a structurally equivalent effect to multi-stage stacking through its single integrated frame. The alternating upper/lower sail attachment (three sails from T, three from B, in alternating sectors) means the effective aerodynamic phase distribution resembles a two-stage, 6-bucket design within one structure.

Helical analogy

The 30° rotational offset between the upper ring (U1–U6) and lower ring (L1–L6) produces a diagonal belt of structural rods that wrap helically around the equatorial zone of the frame. This is geometrically analogous to the helical Savonius variant (developed by Risto Joutsiniemi in the early 1980s), which smooths the torque profile and improves self-starting reliability.

Triangulated frame rigidity

A closed triangulated frame is statically determinate: every member is either in pure tension or pure compression, with no bending loads in the idealized case. The structural load path is:

  1. Wind pressure → sail fabric → three corner nodes of each triangular sail
  2. Node forces → rods in tension/compression
  3. Rod forces → apex hubs → shaft → bearings → support structure

4. Related Commercial Designs

GeoWind GW1200 — the closest existing parallel

The GeoWind GW1200 (CES 2026 Innovation Award, iF Design Award) applies geodesic geometry — an icosahedron frame (20 triangular faces, 12 vertices) — to a VAWT, achieving self-balancing rotation, omnidirectional capture, and a cut-in wind speed of 4 m/s.

VINDSNURR and GW1200 share the core design thesis — that a fully triangulated polyhedral frame provides structural rigidity, load distribution, and aerodynamic geometry simultaneously — but differ in their specific polyhedron. See the geometry comparison for a full analysis.

6. VINDSNURR Sail Logic

Sail geometry

Each sail is a single triangular fabric panel. The three corners attach to three structural nodes:

  • Upper sails (3 off): T–U1–L1, T–U3–L3, T–U5–L5 — hanging diagonally from the top apex
  • Lower sails (3 off): B–L1–U2, B–L3–U4, B–L5–U6 — rising diagonally from the bottom apex

The diagonal edge of each sail (T to an L node, or B to a U node) is a fabric edge only — not a structural stay.

Alternating pattern

  • Sectors 1, 3, 5: upper sails (blue in visualizer)
  • Sectors 2, 4, 6: lower sails (green in visualizer)

All six unsailed sectors are open frame — intentionally, to reduce return-stroke drag.

Rotation direction: Counter-clockwise when viewed from above with wind arriving from the front.

Options for power extraction:

  1. Direct-drive low-RPM alternator — purpose-built axial flux generators wound for 50–200 RPM. No gearbox losses.
  2. Step-up gearbox — increases RPM to match a standard generator. Adds mechanical losses.
  3. Salvaged DC motor as generator — treadmill motors (90–180V DC) work for proof-of-concept.

9. VAWT Performance in Residential Context

  • Residential VAWTs typically range from 400 W to 600 W rated capacity. A 5–15 kW system is needed for meaningful household contribution.
  • Real-world output is typically 15–30% below manufacturer rated figures.
  • Savonius efficiency: 10–17% in practice. Darrieus lift-type VAWTs reach 30–40%. HAWTs reach 40–50%.
  • Wind speed has a cubic effect on output: a site averaging 12 mph produces roughly 70% more power than one averaging 10 mph.
  • VAWTs are particularly well suited to sites with turbulent or rapidly shifting wind — urban rooftops, coastal margins.
