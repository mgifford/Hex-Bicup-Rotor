Generator Selection Guide

Power extraction for a low-RPM drag turbine

Honest assessment: The VINDSNURR is a low-RPM, high-drag, Savonius-style rotor. This heavily constrains generator choice. The question is not "what motor works" but "what generator produces usable power at low RPM without stalling the rotor." All power estimates below are theoretical. No prototype has been tested.

1. The non-negotiable constraint

Based on the rotor geometry and Savonius drag principle (λ ≈ 1, Cp ≈ 0.20), the VINDSNURR will likely produce:

Wind speedEstimated shaft RPMEstimated shaft power
5 m/s (11 mph)~96 RPM~16 W
7 m/s (16 mph)~134 RPM~44 W
10 m/s (22 mph)~191 RPM~128 W
12 m/s (27 mph)~220 RPM~221 W

Shaft power ≠ electrical output. Generator efficiency, cogging losses, rectifier losses, and wiring resistance will reduce these figures by 30–60% in practice. Treat the shaft power estimates as an upper bound, not a target.

Any generator chosen must:

  • Produce usable voltage at 100–200 RPM without gearing, or at 500–1000 RPM with a step-up drive
  • Have low cogging torque — a generator that resists rotation will stall the rotor before it can generate
  • Not require a minimum RPM to start — self-starting is a Savonius advantage; don't design it away

2. Generator options — ranked for this rotor

Option A — Low-KV brushless outrunner motor ⭐ Best starting point

Drone and RC aircraft outrunner motors are widely available, affordable, and designed for rotation. Used as generators, they produce three-phase AC that requires rectification.

PropertyDetail
KV rating to target100–300 KV (lower = more voltage at low RPM)
ExamplesT-Motor U series, Turnigy RotoMax 150cc, large agricultural drone motors
Output at 150 RPMRoughly 5–15V AC before rectification at 150 KV rating
CoggingModerate — present but usually manageable
Cost$30–$150 depending on size
Electronics needed3-phase bridge rectifier, capacitor, voltage regulator

KV rating explained: KV = RPM per volt when used as a motor. As a generator, a 150 KV motor produces roughly 1V per 150 RPM. At 191 RPM you get ~1.3V — too low for direct use. A 50 KV motor would give ~3.8V at the same RPM. Lower KV = more useful at low RPM.

Option B — E-bike hub motor ⭐ Best upgrade path

Direct-drive e-bike hub motors are purpose-built for low-RPM, high-torque operation. They are effectively permanent magnet alternators in a convenient package.

PropertyDetail
KV ratingTypically 5–20 KV equivalent — excellent for low RPM
ExamplesBafang, MAC motor, any 36V or 48V direct-drive hub
Output at 150 RPM10–30V depending on winding, before rectification
CoggingLow to moderate — most are optimised for smooth starting
Cost$80–$300 new; often available secondhand from e-bike conversions
MountingRequires adaptor to connect to rotor shaft

Option C — Permanent Magnet Alternator (PMA) ⭐ Best performance ceiling

Small wind turbine generators — sold as replacement parts for commercial micro-turbines — are optimised for exactly this application: low RPM, variable wind, high torque. They are the most capable option but also the most expensive off-the-shelf, or the most labour-intensive to build.

PropertyDetail
ExamplesPrimus Wind Power replacement alternators, Ginlong / Naier small PMAs
Cut-in RPMTypically 100–200 RPM
Output12V or 24V nominal at rated RPM
CoggingLow — specifically engineered for smooth starting
Cost$200–$600+ off-the-shelf

Option D — Stepper motor ⚠️ Testing only

Stepper motors are often reached for first because they generate voltage with no electronics and are cheap. The problem is severe cogging torque — they resist rotation at every detent position. On a low-torque Savonius rotor this will stall rotation before any useful power is produced.

Use a stepper only to confirm the rotor physically spins before investing in a real generator. Expect it to slow or stop the rotor in any wind below 8–10 m/s.

Option E — Brushed DC motor ⚠️ Early prototyping only

A brushed DC motor wired backwards generates DC directly — no rectifier needed. Easy to demonstrate. However: brushes wear out, efficiency is low (50–70%), and output is inconsistent. Adequate for a first proof-of-concept demonstration, not for sustained operation.

Option F — Custom axial flux generator (advanced)

A flat-disc generator with permanent magnets on a rotor plate and coils on a stator plate is the highest-performance DIY option. Cogging can be nearly eliminated by design. Output voltage and RPM can be tuned by choosing the number of poles and coil turns.

This is a second-phase project. It requires coil winding, magnet sourcing, and either machining or fibreglass casting. Not recommended until the rotor has been validated mechanically.

4. Direct drive vs geared

Direct coupling the generator to the rotor shaft is mechanically simple but requires a very low-KV generator to produce useful voltage at 100–200 RPM. Gearing up the generator RPM relaxes this constraint.

ApproachRatioProsCons
Direct drive1:1No added friction, simple mounting, no belt slipRequires very low-KV generator; limits generator choice
Belt drive3:1 – 8:1Opens up cheaper, higher-KV generators; easy ratio changeBelt friction ~5%, alignment critical, belt wear
Gear train5:1 – 20:1Compact, no slipMore complex, noise, requires lubrication
Friction wheelVariableSimple to buildSlip under load, wear

For a first build: direct drive with a low-KV motor is the simplest. If voltage is insufficient, add a 3:1 belt drive before switching generators.

5. Electronics required

Regardless of generator type, you will need these components between the generator and your load or battery:

ComponentPurposeNotes
3-phase bridge rectifierConverts 3-phase AC (BLDC/PMA) to DCUse 35A bridge rectifier module; size for peak current
Smoothing capacitorReduces voltage ripple after rectification1000–4700 µF electrolytic at appropriate voltage rating
Voltage regulator / MPPT controllerRegulates variable generator output to stable charging voltageA wind charge controller (not solar MPPT) is preferred; handles variable-frequency AC input
Dump loadDissipates power when battery is fullCritical for battery safety; typically a resistive heater element
BatteryStores energy for use when wind drops12V or 24V lead-acid or LiFePO4; size to daily generation estimate

Minimum test circuit (Phase 1–2): Generator → 3-phase rectifier → LED string or USB power meter. This confirms generation without risk to electronics.

6. Realistic power expectations

This is the most important section. The numbers below are honest estimates, not marketing figures.

ScenarioRealistic electrical output
5 m/s average wind, direct drive, low-KV BLDC5–10 W electrical
7 m/s average wind, belt drive 3:1, BLDC15–25 W electrical
10 m/s average wind, e-bike hub motor40–80 W electrical
10 m/s average wind, custom PMA60–100 W electrical

What these outputs can power:

  • 5–10 W: LED lighting, sensors, low-power microcontrollers (Arduino, Raspberry Pi Zero)
  • 15–25 W: USB device charging, small battery maintenance
  • 40–80 W: Laptop charging, 12V appliances, meaningful battery storage

What this turbine will not power at typical residential wind speeds: household appliances, heating, air conditioning, or a meaningful fraction of household electricity consumption. A 5–15 kW system would be needed for that — roughly 5–15× this rotor's diameter.

The VINDSNURR is optimised for turbulence handling, low cut-in speed, and aesthetic form — not peak power extraction. The generator must adapt to that reality.

8. One key insight

The VINDSNURR is not optimised for peak efficiency. It is optimised for omnidirectional wind capture, smooth torque in turbulent conditions, and structural elegance. The generator choice must reflect that — a high-efficiency generator that demands high RPM or high starting torque will fight the rotor rather than work with it.

The right mental model: treat the rotor as a low-speed, high-torque prime mover, and select the generator accordingly. Torque is available; RPM is limited. Low-KV motors and PMAs are designed exactly for this trade-off.

All power figures are theoretical estimates. Generator efficiency, bearing friction, rectifier losses, and real wind variability will reduce actual output. Validate with a power meter before sizing batteries or loads.
